## The work

I [went into self employment](https://csswizardry.com/2013/10/lets-work-together/) in typical _me_ fashion: informal, optimistic, freestyle. My thinking was that I‚Äôd had 6‚Äì7 years of amazing but very predictable employment, so **I wanted to make the most of striking out alone by not having any rigid plans in place**. This worked out very, _very_ well. The sheer variety and amount of work I‚Äôve been able to do as a result has been astounding. In the last year I‚Äôve worked for large companies like [the BBC](https://csswizardry.com/case-studies/bbc-workshop/) and [the NHS](https://csswizardry.com/case-studies/nhs-nhsx-elearning-platform/), to [smaller companies](https://csswizardry.com/case-studies/madgex-consultancy-workshop/), to working with [individuals](https://csswizardry.com/services/#quote:markus-klug) from their home offices.

The single longest engagement I had was with [the NHS](https://csswizardry.com/case-studies/nhs-nhsx-elearning-platform/), and lasted just 21 days. The majority of my work has been single-day workshops with companies, and a few week-to-fortnight engagements with several clients who required a little more involvement.

The vast majority of my work has been consultancy and internal workshops: heading into companies for short (often just one day) stints and assessing, troubleshooting, teaching, and helping teams manage large and/or growing codebases. **This has been immensely rewarding and is exactly the kind of thing I wanted to do.**

I have only worked on three development projects in the last year. One was an initial sprint‚Äôs worth of work to define and plan out architectures and strategies; another was [the NHS project](https://csswizardry.com/case-studies/nhs-nhsx-elearning-platform/) which was several sprint‚Äôs worth of work handled in a very satellite fashion; another is still ongoing. I‚Äôve really enjoyed the dev work, but only because my role has, again, been very consultancy led. **I‚Äôve turned down every project that has had already-finished designs** and just needed someone to build them, because that is just a fundamentally broken way of working, and something I really do not want to encourage or participate in.

A worry I‚Äôd had about stepping away from day-to-day development work and going consultancy was about falling behind the curve and losing the real-world experiences of being a developer. What ended up happening was the complete opposite: as a result of parachuting into companies for a day or two at a time I was exposed to a lot more varied and diverse problems than I would have done if I‚Äôd been working on dev projects. **Spending a little bit of time with a lot of very different companies meant that I was solving more problems than ever before**, and this is great news.

The types of projects and companies have been very varied as well, which is something I knew that I really wanted from day one. The reason I‚Äôve not taken the contracting or freelance route is because I don‚Äôt want to work on the same projects for months on end at the expense of variety. ## Year 2

More of the same, please!
